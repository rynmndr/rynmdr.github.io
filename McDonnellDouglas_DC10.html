McDonnell Douglas DC-10

    McDonnell Douglas DC-10 Features

  • Trijet Configuration: The DC-10 featured a unique trijet configuration, providing redundancy and operational flexibility.

  • Capacity: The DC-10 was available in several variants with seating capacities typically ranging from 250 to 380 passengers.

  • Wide-Body Design: The DC-10 was a wide-body aircraft, providing more space for passengers and cargo.

  • Range: The DC-10 had the capability to cover long-haul routes, with a range of approximately 3,800 to 6,600 nautical miles, depending on the variant.

The McDonnell Douglas DC-10 is an American trijet wide-body aircraft first introduced by American Airlines in 1971. It was designed to succeed the DC-8 for long-range flights.

The trijet had two turbofans on underwing pylons and a third one at the base of the vertical stabilizer. It had a typical seating capacity for 270 passengers in two classes. The DC-10 came in various models, including the DC-10-10, DC-10-15, DC-10-30, and DC-10-40, each with different ranges.

The DC-10 also served as the basis for the KC-10 Extender, a tanker aircraft operated by the United States Air Force.

The DC-10's development was influenced by the need to compete in the widebody aircraft market and pressure to produce a widebody aircraft quickly.
